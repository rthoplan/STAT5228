The Visual Perception Masterclass

Comparing "Bad" vs "Good" design patterns to understand human data decoding.

Practical Design Rules

01 Direct Labeling

Place labels next to data points. Eliminate the back-and-forth scanning between a legend and the plot.

02 Zero Clutter

Aggressively remove non-data ink. Fewer grid lines, muted borders, and no heavy backgrounds.

03 Small Multiples

Break overloaded charts into a series of simple, consistent plots for easier cross-comparison.

04 Flatten Everything

Never use 3D or perspective. They distort proportions and make statistical comparison impossible.

05 Strategic Color

Color is for highlighting specific insights, not for decoration. If every bar is a different color, none are special.

06 Accessibility First

Ensure high contrast and colorblind-safe palettes. Use texture or labels as secondary identifiers.

2. Statistical Color Logic

How poor color choices mislead the mathematical brain.

❌ Bad: The Rainbow Trap

Rainbow Scale for Numbers

The Issue: Rainbow scales (Jet/Spectral) introduce "false boundaries." Yellow appears brighter than Green, making it look like a peak when the data is actually smooth. It fails color-blind users entirely.

✅ Good: Single-Hue Sequential

Lightness Scale for Numbers

The Benefit: Perceptually uniform. The brain naturally understands that "darker = more." This remains interpretable even in grayscale or for color-blind viewers.

❌ Bad: False Order

Gradient for Categories

The Issue: Using a single-hue gradient for categories (like Departments) incorrectly implies that Dept D is "greater" or "better" than Dept A. It confuses identity with magnitude.
